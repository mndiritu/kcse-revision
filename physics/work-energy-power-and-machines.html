WORK, ENERGY, POWER & MACHINES ‚Äî WRITING NOTES (KCSE)

1) Big picture: what you must be able to do

This topic is basically ‚Äúhow energy moves around‚Äù and ‚Äúhow machines make work easier‚Äù. You must be able to:

  • Define work, energy and power + give SI units.
  • Use key formulas: W = Fd, P = W/t, P.E = mgh, K.E = ¬Ωmv¬≤, EPE = ¬Ωke¬≤.
  • Use the Law of Conservation of Energy and describe energy transformations.
  • For machines: use M.A, V.R, efficiency, and explain why efficiency is always < 100%.

Quick exam language (use these exact phrases)

  • Work is done when a force causes movement in the direction of the force.
  • Power is the rate of doing work (or rate of energy conversion).
  • Efficiency is useful output divided by input, as a percentage.
2) Sources of Energy (renewable vs non-renewable)

Energy sources are classified into:

  • Renewable: supply can be renewed again and again (e.g. water, solar, wind, geothermal).
  • Non-renewable: supply cannot be renewed again and again (e.g. fossils, firewood, nuclear).

Low-resource classroom examples

  • Solar: phone solar charger / solar lamp.
  • Wind: windmill video / paper pinwheel demo.
  • Biogas / firewood: common household comparison (energy + environmental impact).
3) Forms of Energy

The common forms include:

  • Mechanical (Potential + Kinetic)
  • Chemical (stored in batteries and food)
  • Electrical
  • Light
  • Nuclear
  • Wave

Note: Potential energy is energy due to position or state, while kinetic energy is energy due to motion.

4) Law of Conservation of Energy + Energy Transformations

Law of Conservation of Energy: Energy can neither be created nor destroyed but can only be transformed from one form to another.

Energy transformation and transducers

A device that helps transform energy from one form to another is called a transducer.

  • Solar ‚Üí Heat: solar panel
  • Electrical ‚Üí Kinetic: motor
  • Kinetic ‚Üí Electrical: dynamo
  • Solar ‚Üí Electrical: solar cell
  • Heat ‚Üí Electrical: thermocouple
  • Electrical ‚Üí Sound: loudspeaker
  • Chemical ‚Üí Electrical: battery

KCSE-style transformation statement (example)

If a hammer hits lead and its temperature rises: kinetic energy ‚Üí heat energy.

5) Work (W)

Definition: Work is the product of force and distance moved in the direction of the force.

Formula: W = F √ó d
SI unit: joule (J)
1 J = 1 N m

Important KCSE point: No work is done if there is no movement in the direction of the force (e.g. pushing a wall; carrying a load on the head while standing still).

Worked examples (copy this structure)

Example A: lifting a stone

Mass = 15 kg, height = 2.0 m, take g = 10 N kg‚àí1.

  • Force = weight = mg = 15 √ó 10 = 150 N
  • Work done = Fd = 150 √ó 2 = 300 J

Example B: climbing stairs

Mass = 40 kg, 12 steps, each 20 cm high.

  • Total height = 12 √ó 20/100 = 2.4 m
  • Work done = mgh = 40 √ó 10 √ó 2.4 = 960 J
Work from a force‚Äìdistance graph (area under the graph)

Key rule: The area under a force‚Äìdistance graph represents work done (energy spent).

6) Gravitational Potential Energy (P.E)

Definition: Energy possessed due to height above a surface. The P.E gained equals the work done in raising the body.

Formula: P.E = mgh

7) Elastic Potential Energy (spring energy)

This is energy stored in a stretched or compressed spring. It equals the work done in stretching/compressing.

Key ideas and derivation (KCSE-short)

  • Work done = average force √ó extension
  • Average force = (0 + F)/2 = F/2
  • So work done = (F/2) √ó e
  • But Hooke‚Äôs law: F = ke
  • Therefore: Elastic P.E = ¬Ωke¬≤
Worked example: spring extension

Force 7.5 N stretches a spring by 5 cm. Find work done in stretching by 8 cm.

  • Force for 8 cm: 7.5 √ó 8/5 = 12.0 N
  • Work done = ¬Ω F e = ¬Ω √ó 12.0 √ó 8/100 = 0.48 J
8) Kinetic Energy (K.E)

Definition: Energy possessed due to motion.

Formula: K.E = ¬Ωmv¬≤

KCSE meaning: work done to accelerate a body = kinetic energy gained.

Worked example: trolley pulled by a force

Mass 2.0 kg, pulled from rest by force 5.0 N for 1.2 s, no friction.

  • Acceleration: a = F/m = 5/2 = 2.5 m s‚àí2
  • Distance: s = ¬Ωat¬≤ = ¬Ω √ó 2.5 √ó (1.2)¬≤ = 1.8 m
  • K.E gained = work done = Fs = 5 √ó 1.8 = 9.0 J
Worked example: braking force and work

Speed 72 km h‚àí1 (= 20 m s‚àí1), stops in 8 s, mass 1250 kg.

  • a = (0 ‚àí 20)/8 = ‚àí2.5 m s‚àí2
  • Braking force magnitude: F = ma = 1250 √ó 2.5 = 3125 N
  • Work done to stop = kinetic energy lost = ¬Ωmu¬≤
9) Interchange of P.E and K.E (projected body / falling body)

For a body projected vertically upwards (air resistance negligible):

  • As it rises: K.E decreases, P.E increases.
  • At the top: K.E = 0, P.E is maximum.
  • As it falls: P.E decreases, K.E increases.

Total mechanical energy: E = P.E + K.E = constant (if no energy losses).

10) Power

Definition: Power is the rate of doing work (work done per unit time). Since work done equals energy converted, power is also the rate of energy conversion.

Formula: P = W / t or P = Energy converted / time
SI unit: watt (W) where 1 W = 1 J s‚àí1

Worked examples (typical KCSE)

Example: climbing stairs

Weight 500 N, height 3.0 m, time 4 s

  • Work done = Fd = 500 √ó 3 = 1500 J
  • Power = W/t = 1500/4 = 375 W

Example: motor lifting a load

Mass 50 kg, height 24 m, time 40 s, g = 10 N kg‚àí1

  • Work = mgh = 50 √ó 10 √ó 24 = 12000 J
  • Power = 12000/40 = 300 W

Shortcut you can mention (constant speed)

Since v = d/t, then P = (F√ód)/t = Fv (for uniform velocity).

11) Machines: Effort, Load, M.A, V.R, Efficiency

A machine is a device that enables work to be done more easily or conveniently. A force applied at one point (effort) is used to overcome a load at another point.

Core terms (definitions + units)

  • Effort (E): force applied to the machine (unit: N)
  • Load (L): force exerted by the machine / force overcome (unit: N)
  • Mechanical Advantage (M.A): M.A = Load / Effort (no unit)
  • Velocity Ratio (V.R): V.R = DE / DL (no unit)
  • Efficiency (Œ∑): Œ∑ = (Work output / Work input) √ó 100%

Most tested machine identity (memorise)

Since Work output = L√óDL and Work input = E√óDE:
Œ∑ = (L√óDL)/(E√óDE) √ó 100% = (M.A / V.R) √ó 100%

Factors affecting M.A and efficiency
  • Friction between moving parts (more friction ‚Üí lower M.A and lower efficiency).
  • Weight of parts lifted (heavier moving parts ‚Üí lower M.A and lower efficiency).

This is why efficiency is always less than 100% for real machines.

12) Simple machines (formulas for V.R)
A) Levers

A lever works using the principle of moments. It has a pivot (fulcrum), effort arm (EA), load arm (LA).

V.R of a lever: V.R = EA / LA

Lever sketch

B) Wheel and axle

A large wheel of radius R is fixed to an axle of radius r. Effort applied at wheel; load attached to axle.

V.R: V.R = R / r

Example (KCSE pattern)

Load 300 N, effort 50 N, wheel radius 0.85 m, axle radius 0.10 m.

  • M.A = L/E = 300/50 = 6
  • V.R = R/r = 0.85/0.10 = 8.5
  • Œ∑ = (M.A/V.R)√ó100 = (6/8.5)√ó100 = 70.6% (approx.)
C) Inclined plane

If the plane makes angle Œ∏ with horizontal:

V.R: V.R = Effort distance / Load distance = L / h = 1 / sinŒ∏

Example (effort + friction work)

Lift 50 kg through vertical height 4.0 m, plane angle 30¬∞, efficiency 72%, g = 10 N kg‚àí1.

  • V.R = 1/sin30¬∞ = 2
  • M.A = (Œ∑/100)√óV.R = 0.72√ó2 = 1.44
  • Load = mg = 50√ó10 = 500 N
  • Effort = Load/M.A = 500/1.44 = 347.2 N
  • Work output = mgh = 50√ó10√ó4 = 2000 J
  • Effort distance along plane = h/sin30¬∞ = 4/0.5 = 8 m
  • Work input = effort √ó distance = 347.2√ó8 = 2777.6 J
  • Work against friction = input ‚àí output = 2777.6 ‚àí 2000 = 777.6 J
D) Screw (jack-screw)

The distance between successive threads is the pitch. In one complete revolution, the screw advances by one pitch.

V.R of screw: V.R = (circumference of handle circle) / pitch = 2œÄR / pitch

E) Gears

A gear is a wheel with teeth. If driver has n teeth and driven has N teeth:

V.R of gear system: V.R = (teeth on driven) / (teeth on driver) = N / n

F) Pulleys

A pulley is a wheel with a groove for a rope/string. Key rule: V.R is the number of ropes supporting the load.

  • Single fixed pulley: V.R = 1
  • Single movable pulley: V.R = 2
  • Block and tackle: V.R depends on number of supporting rope segments (commonly 3, 4, 5, 6...)

Simple pulley sketch (fixed pulley)

Why pulley systems are not 100% efficient
  • Energy is lost in overcoming friction between rope and pulley.
  • Energy is lost in lifting the movable pulleys (their weight matters).
  • For an odd number of pulleys, it is convenient to have more fixed than movable to reduce losses.
G) Pulley belts

If driver radius is R and driven radius is r, then:

V.R: V.R = radius of driven / radius of driver = r / R

H) Hydraulic machines

In hydraulic machines, volume is conserved: (distance moved by effort piston √ó area of effort piston) = (distance moved by load piston √ó area of load piston).

If load piston radius = R and effort piston radius = r, then:
V.R = R¬≤ / r¬≤

Example (effort + energy wasted)

Effort piston radius = 1.4 cm, load piston radius = 7.0 cm, load = 120 kg, height = 2.5 m, efficiency = 80%, g = 10 N kg‚àí1.

  • V.R = (7/1.4)¬≤ = (5)¬≤ = 25
  • M.A = (Œ∑/100)√óV.R = 0.8√ó25 = 20
  • Load = mg = 120√ó10 = 1200 N
  • Effort = Load/M.A = 1200/20 = 60 N
  • Work output = mgh = 120√ó10√ó2.5 = 3000 J
  • Work input = output√ó100/80 = 3000√ó100/80 = 3750 J
  • Energy wasted = input ‚àí output = 3750 ‚àí 3000 = 750 J

1) One-line definitions (write them like this)

  • Work: product of force and distance moved in the direction of the force.
  • Energy: capacity to do work.
  • Power: rate of doing work / rate of energy conversion.
  • Machine: device that makes work easier by transferring force.

2) KCSE traps (avoid losing easy marks)

  • If there is no displacement in direction of force, work done is zero.
  • Always include SI units: J, W, N, m, s.
  • Efficiency is a percentage. Real machines: Œ∑ < 100% because of losses.
  • For pulleys: don‚Äôt guess. Count rope segments supporting the load.
  • For inclined planes: make sure you use sinŒ∏ correctly.
