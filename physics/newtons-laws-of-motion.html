NEWTON‚ÄôS LAWS OF MOTION ‚Äî WRITING NOTES (KCSE)

1) Overview: What this topic is about

This topic explains how forces affect motion. You must know: Newton‚Äôs 1st law (Inertia), 2nd law (F = ma), 3rd law (Action‚ÄìReaction), plus momentum, impulse, conservation of momentum and common applications.

Key exam language

  • Resultant force = net force (sum of forces).
  • Acceleration is change of velocity per time: a = (v ‚àí u)/t.
  • Momentum measures ‚Äúquantity of motion‚Äù: p = mv.
  • Impulse links force and time: Impulse = Ft = change in momentum (Œîp).
2) Newton‚Äôs First Law (Law of Inertia)

Statement: A body remains at rest or moves with uniform velocity in a straight line unless acted upon by an external resultant force.

Inertia (Definition)

Inertia is the tendency of a body to resist change in its state of motion. A moving body ‚Äúresists stopping‚Äù, and a stationary body ‚Äúresists starting to move‚Äù.

Everyday examples (KCSE-friendly)

  • When a moving bus stops suddenly, passengers lurch forward (they tend to keep moving).
  • When a bus starts suddenly, passengers jerk backward (they resist motion).
  • Seat belts and airbags reduce injuries by controlling effects of inertia.
3) Momentum

Definition: Momentum is the product of mass and velocity.

Formula: p = mv

SI unit: kg m s‚àí1

Nature: Momentum is a vector (has magnitude and direction). Direction is same as velocity.

Worked Example (pattern you can copy)

Example: A van of mass 3 metric tonnes travels at 72 km h‚àí1. Find momentum.

  • m = 3 √ó 103 kg
  • v = 72 km h‚àí1 = 20 m s‚àí1
  • p = mv = 3 √ó 103 √ó 20 = 6 √ó 104 kg m s‚àí1
4) Newton‚Äôs Second Law (Force‚ÄìMass‚ÄìAcceleration)

Statement: The rate of change of momentum is directly proportional to the resultant external force, and occurs in the direction of that force.

Derivation you should remember (short)

Change in momentum = mv ‚àí mu
Rate of change = (mv ‚àí mu)/t
But acceleration a = (v ‚àí u)/t
Therefore, F ‚àù ma and the constant becomes 1 in SI units:

F = ma

Definition of a newton (N): 1 N is the force that gives 1 kg an acceleration of 1 m s‚àí2.

Typical KCSE calculations

  • Use F = ma when force is constant and acceleration is known.
  • Use a = (v ‚àí u)/t to get acceleration/retardation from speed change.

Example (braking force)

A car of mass 1200 kg travelling at 45 m s‚àí1 stops in 9 s.

  • a = (0 ‚àí 45)/9 = ‚àí5 m s‚àí2
  • F = ma = 1200 √ó (‚àí5) = ‚àí6000 N (retarding force)
5) Impulse

Definition: Impulse is the product of force and the time for which it acts.

Impulse: I = Ft

SI unit: newton-second (N s)

From Newton‚Äôs 2nd law: F = (mv ‚àí mu)/t ‚áí Ft = mv ‚àí mu = Œîp
So Impulse = change in momentum.

Why airbags/seatbelts help (KCSE explanation)

During a collision, momentum changes quickly. If the time of impact is made longer, the impulsive force becomes smaller, reducing damage. Airbags, seatbelts, collapsible bumpers increase collision time and reduce force.

6) Newton‚Äôs Third Law (Action‚ÄìReaction)

Statement: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. If body P exerts a force on body Q, then Q exerts an equal and opposite force on P.

  • Walking: foot pushes ground backward; ground pushes you forward.
  • Jumping from a boat: you push the boat backward; boat pushes you forward (boat moves back due to low friction with water).
  • Block on table: block pushes table down; table pushes block up (reaction).
7) Apparent weight in a lift (elevator)

A weighing machine reads the reaction force from the lift floor. This can be different from true weight depending on acceleration.

Results you must know

  • Lift at rest / constant velocity: P = mg
  • Lift accelerating upward (a): P = mg + ma (you feel heavier)
  • Lift accelerating downward (a): P = mg ‚àí ma (you feel lighter)
  • If a = g downward: P = 0 (weightlessness)
8) Conservation of Linear Momentum

Law: For a system of colliding bodies, total linear momentum remains constant provided no external forces act.

m1u1 + m2u2 = m1v1 + m2v2

Choose a positive direction first (e.g., to the right). Velocities opposite that direction are negative.

9) Elastic vs Inelastic collisions

Elastic collision

  • Momentum conserved
  • Kinetic energy conserved
  • Bodies separate after collision.

Inelastic collision

  • Momentum conserved
  • Kinetic energy NOT conserved (some becomes heat/sound/deformation)
  • In a perfectly inelastic collision, bodies coalesce and move together with a common velocity.
10) Recoil and propulsion (applications)

Gun recoil

Initially, total momentum is zero. After firing, bullet momentum forward equals gun momentum backward (equal and opposite).

Rocket and jet propulsion

Exhaust gases are pushed out at high speed, gaining momentum in one direction. Rocket gains equal momentum in opposite direction (thrust).

Garden sprinkler

Water ejected through nozzles gains momentum; sprinkler rotates due to reaction.

11) Frictional force (as covered in the notes)

Friction is a force that opposes relative motion (or tendency to move) between surfaces in contact. It acts opposite the direction of motion or pulling force.

Types

  • Static/limiting friction (Fs): force needed to just start motion
    Fs = ŒºsR
  • Kinetic/sliding friction (Fk): force needed to keep a body moving at constant speed
    Fk = ŒºkR

Note: Œºs > Œºk

Laws of friction (summary)

  1. Friction opposes relative motion.
  2. Independent of area of contact (for constant R).
  3. Proportional to normal reaction R.
  4. Kinetic friction independent of relative speed (approx.).
  5. Depends on nature of surfaces.
