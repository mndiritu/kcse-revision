Colonial Administration

European powers set up administrative systems in Africa mainly to control people and resources. Learn the systems, how they worked, and the effects — then drill with KCSE questions.

Definition (memorise)

Colonial administration is the system used by European powers to govern African colonies after partition, mainly to maintain control and enable exploitation of resources.

Systems of Colonial Administration
  • Indirect Rule (mainly British; also used by Germans, Dutch, Italians in some areas)
  • Direct Rule (used where settlers were many or Africans resisted strongly)
  • Assimilation (mainly French; also used by Belgians & Portuguese)
  • Association (French adjustment when assimilation proved impractical)
Indirect Rule: Meaning

A system where African traditional rulers (chiefs) were used to administer Africans under the supervision and instructions of British officials.

Why the British used Indirect Rule
  • Cost saving: Britain had limited funds for colonial administration.
  • Shortage of manpower: few Europeans wanted to work in tropical Africa (disease/hardship fears).
  • Reduce resistance: made Africans feel their leaders still had power.
  • Past success: had worked in places like India and Uganda, encouraging use elsewhere.
  • Administrative realities: easier where centralized leadership already existed.
Indirect Rule in Kenya (how it worked)
  • Used where possible, especially among communities with recognised leaders (e.g., Wanga, Maasai).
  • Where no strong chiefly institution existed, the British appointed chiefs (often based on ability to communicate or assist administrators).

Examples of appointed chiefs (Kenya)

  • Kinyanjui wa Gathirimu (Kiambu)
  • Wang’ombe wa Ihura (Nyeri)
  • Karuri wa Gakure (Murang’a)
  • Musau wa Mwanza (Ukambani)
  • Nthiwa wa Tama (Ukambani)

Duties of African chiefs (Kenya)

  • Maintain public order
  • Hear minor/petty cases
  • Clear roads and footpaths
  • Control cultivation of “poisonous” crops (e.g., bhang)
  • Assist District Officers in tax collection
  • Control illegal liquor brewing
  • Control carrying of weapons

Note: Chiefs could be fined if they failed in these duties.

Why many chiefs were unpopular

  • Lacked legitimacy (many were not traditional leaders).
  • Seen as tools of colonial oppression and exploitation.
  • Some used positions to enrich themselves (land, livestock, wives).
Colonial administrative structure in Kenya (memorise hierarchy)
  • Secretary of State for Colonies (London)
  • Governor / High Commissioner (Nairobi)
  • Provincial Commissioner (PC)
  • District Commissioner (DC)
  • District Officer (DO)
  • Chiefs
  • Headmen

Most offices above Chief were held by Europeans.

Effects of Indirect Rule in Kenya
  • Encouraged divide and rule and creation of isolated tribal reserves.
  • Chiefs enforced colonial laws regardless of impact on Africans.
  • Created misrule in some areas (especially where chiefs were imposed on stateless societies).
  • Led to unpopular colonial laws being pushed through local intermediaries.
Indirect Rule in Northern Nigeria (Lugard)
  • Nigeria originally had separate units (Lagos, Southern Protectorate, Northern Protectorate).
  • Lagos joined Southern Nigeria (1906); Northern and Southern merged (1914) to form Nigeria.
  • Frederick Lugard used indirect rule in the North through the Emirs under Islamic law.
  • British Residents supervised Emir courts to reduce abuse of power.
  • North divided into provinces under Residents (PCs) and districts under DOs; Emirs worked under DO supervision.

Why indirect rule worked better in Northern Nigeria

  • Existing centralized emirate system under Sokoto Caliphate.
  • Helped reduce administrative costs and manpower needs.
  • Reduced resistance by keeping local leadership structure.
Why indirect rule failed in Southern Nigeria
  • Many ethnic groups and less centralized political systems.
  • Resistance to tax led to riots (e.g., 1918 and 1929 noted in the material).
  • Appointment of “warrant chiefs” and mission-educated young men caused resentment.
  • Lack of linguistic unity made administration difficult.
  • Educated elites opposed illiterate/appointed chiefs.
General effects of indirect rule
  • Made chiefs unpopular (tax collection, labour recruitment, enforcing colonial rules).
  • Encouraged exploitative policies: land alienation, forced labour, taxation.
  • Created mistrust between educated elites and traditional chiefs.
  • Slow development in some areas due to conservatism/resistance to new ideas.
  • Preserved some African cultural institutions more than assimilation did.
Direct Rule: Meaning

A system where the colonial power ruled directly through European officials. Indigenous institutions and leaders were replaced or weakened.

Direct Rule in Zimbabwe (Southern Rhodesia)
  • Introduced by the British South Africa Company (BSAC) of Cecil Rhodes.
  • Settler domination: many whites settled, occupied fertile lands and shaped administration.
  • Legislative Council started early (1898), dominated by settlers; Africans not represented.
  • Land alienation and forced labour used to support settler economy.

Reasons for direct rule in Zimbabwe

  • Settlers wanted full economic control (mineral wealth and farmland).
  • Resistance (e.g., Shona and Ndebele wars; Chimurenga uprising) reduced trust in traditional leadership.
  • Many Europeans available to fill administrative posts.
  • Desire for complete control over African communities.

Effects of direct rule in Zimbabwe

  • Traditional leaders lost political power and independence.
  • Land alienation: Africans pushed to reserves; poverty increased.
  • Forced labour and taxation expanded.
  • Racial segregation increased socially, politically, and economically.
  • Nationalism grew due to exploitation; struggle intensified later (UDI and armed resistance).
Land Apportionment Act (1930) — key points
  • Introduced rigid territorial segregation (white areas vs African areas).
  • Whites took large portions of best arable land; Africans moved to harsher reserves.
  • Land categories included: Native Reserves, Native Purchase Areas, European Areas, Unassigned Areas.

Result: land shortages forced Africans into migrant labour in mines, towns, and settler farms.

Industrial Conciliation Act (1934) — key idea
  • Protected white workers from African competition.
  • Helped maintain Africans at the lowest economic levels (cheap manual labour).
Central African Federation (CAF), 1953–1963 — overview
  • Formed in 1953: Southern Rhodesia (Zimbabwe), Northern Rhodesia (Zambia), Nyasaland (Malawi).
  • Each territory kept local government; federal parliament handled shared matters and foreign affairs.
  • Opposed by Africans; reforms attempted but later repression increased.
  • Dissolved in 1963; later Zambia and Malawi became independent; Zimbabwe’s struggle continued.
Assimilation: Meaning

A French policy aiming to make Africans adopt French culture and become “like French citizens” (language, religion, laws, and lifestyle).

Assimilation in Senegal (French West Africa)
  • Strongly linked to French Revolution ideas (liberty and rights), but applied selectively.
  • Administration was centralized from Dakar (capital of French West Africa).
  • Applied most fully in the four communes: St. Louis, Goree, Rufisque, Dakar.
  • Some Africans in communes gained political rights and representation (Deputies in France).

Features of assimilation (memorise 4)

  1. Administrative: colonies treated as overseas provinces of France
  2. Political: representation in French Chamber of Deputies
  3. Economic: integration with French economy (including currency links)
  4. Personal: French citizenship (education, French language, Christianity, civil service)
Why assimilation succeeded (in the four communes)
  • Long interaction with Europeans (traders, missionaries, administrators).
  • High Christian conversion in towns.
  • Presence of Mulatto population (mixed parentage) noted in the material.
Why assimilation failed (generally)
  • Many African communities had intact cultures and little contact with Europeans.
  • French economic interests: hard to exploit people treated as “equal citizens”.
  • Too expensive (schools, hospitals, services needed for “citizens”).
  • Racial discrimination: many French did not accept Africans as equals.
  • Religious resistance (especially Muslims) to forced conversion.
  • Political fears: French citizens feared being outnumbered in parliament.
Consequences of assimilation
  • Undermined African cultures (French language and customs promoted).
  • Eroded authority of traditional rulers (some replaced by assimilated Africans).
  • Created division between assimilated Africans and the rest (who faced forced labour/taxation).
  • Some Africans participated in French politics (e.g., deputies mentioned in the notes).
Association: Idea (French adjustment)

An adjustment to assimilation: subjects were allowed to develop more within their own cultures, because full assimilation proved impractical and discriminatory.

Comparison: British vs French Administration

Similarities

  • Europeans held senior government positions (e.g., Governors).
  • Africans faced oppressive colonial laws and limited political rights.
  • Both used chiefs (appointed or traditional) in areas lacking centralized authority.
  • Both implemented exploitative measures: land alienation, forced labour, taxation.

Differences

  • British: varied administration (direct/indirect); colonies ruled separately by governors.
  • French: more centralized; colonies organized as federations and tied closely to France.
  • British: relied more on traditional rulers (where possible).
  • French: aimed to replace African culture through assimilation; selected “qualified” Africans.
  • French: colonies could elect deputies to Paris; British colonies were not represented in UK parliament.

Quick Revision Sheet (copy fast, revise faster)

  • Colonial administration: systems used by Europeans to control colonies for exploitation.
  • 4 systems: Indirect Rule, Direct Rule, Assimilation, Association.
Indirect Rule (British)
  • Used African chiefs to govern under British supervision.
  • Why used: cheap, fewer Europeans, reduced resistance, worked elsewhere before.
  • Kenya: appointed chiefs where none existed; chiefs collected tax, enforced laws.
  • N. Nigeria: worked well due to centralized emirate system under Islamic law.
  • S. Nigeria: failed due to lack of centralized system, ethnic diversity, resistance to taxes, “warrant chiefs”.
Direct Rule
  • Europeans rule directly; African institutions weakened or replaced.
  • Zimbabwe: settler domination, land alienation, forced labour, racial segregation.
  • Key laws: Land Apportionment Act (1930), Industrial Conciliation Act (1934).
Assimilation (French)
  • Make Africans adopt French culture (language, laws, religion, lifestyle).
  • Applied mainly in Senegal’s four communes (St Louis, Goree, Rufisque, Dakar).
  • Features (4): Administrative, Political, Economic, Personal.
  • Failed generally: expensive, racism, cultural resistance, economic motives, fear of political outnumbering.
British vs French (exam-friendly)
  • Similarities: Europeans senior posts; oppressive laws; taxation/forced labour/land alienation.
  • Differences: British more flexible & colony-by-colony; French centralized & assimilation-oriented.

Common KCSE traps

  • Don’t mix up Indirect Rule (use chiefs) with Direct Rule (Europeans rule directly).
  • Assimilation was mainly French; indirect rule mainly British.
  • Explain failure/success using political structure + culture + cost + resistance.

Practice “10-mark” prompts

  1. Describe how indirect rule was applied in Kenya.
  2. Explain why indirect rule succeeded in Northern Nigeria but failed in Southern Nigeria.
  3. Describe direct rule in Zimbabwe and its effects on Africans.
  4. Explain features of assimilation and why it failed in most colonies.
  5. Compare British and French colonial administration.
