CO-OPERATION IN AFRICA (FORM 4 NOTES)

1) Meaning of Co-operation in Africa
  • Co-operation means African countries/peoples working together to solve shared problems and improve development.
  • It has appeared through Pan-Africanism, OAU/AU, and regional bodies like EAC, ECOWAS, COMESA.
2) PAN-AFRICANISM

(a) Definition

  • ‚ÄúPan‚Äù = all; ‚ÄúAfrican‚Äù = people of African origin.
  • Pan-Africanism is a movement for unity of all people of African descent worldwide, aiming at social, economic, cultural and political advancement.

(b) Factors that led to Pan-Africanism

  1. Trans-Atlantic slave trade
    • Africans were captured, transported and mistreated in the Americas and Caribbean.
  2. Colonial oppression in Africa
    • Land alienation, taxation, forced labour, discrimination, corporal punishment, rape and murder, poor wages.
  3. Racial discrimination in the diaspora
    • Blacks faced segregation and denial of rights (especially in USA and Europe).

(c) Aims/Objectives of Pan-Africanism

  • Unite Africans to struggle against colonial rule and racial discrimination.
  • Challenge white supremacy ideology.
  • Improve conditions of Africans in the diaspora and Africa.
  • Restore dignity and pride of black people.
  • Create a forum to protest colonization and discrimination.
  • Fight neo-colonialism (indirect control of poor countries by rich countries).
3) KEY PAN-AFRICAN LEADERS (SELECT NOTES)

(a) Marcus Garvey

  • Jamaican; major activist in USA from 1916.
  • Preached pride in black identity and culture; supported ‚ÄúAfrica for Africans‚Äù.
  • Formed UNIA (Universal Negro Improvement Association).
  • Promoted black businesses and self-reliance.
  • Arrested/convicted (fraud accusations), jailed then deported; died 1940.

Objectives of UNIA

  1. Universal unity among the black race
  2. Uplift African communities
  3. Establish a central nation for blacks
  4. Promote education for African children
  5. Promote African culture

(b) Booker T. Washington

  • Focused on technical/industrial education and working with government (‚Äúaccommodation‚Äù policy).
  • Founded Tuskegee Institute (industrial training).
  • Helped set up National Negro Business League.

(c) W.E.B. Du Bois

  • Scholar and activist; opposed discrimination.
  • Formed Niagara Movement (1905).
  • Helped organize Pan-African conferences (1900‚Äì1945).
  • Invited to Ghana by Nkrumah (1961); died 1963.

(d) Kwame Nkrumah

  • Ghanaian leader; key Pan-African figure after 1945.
  • Led Ghana to independence (1957) and supported liberation of other African states.
  • Hosted major meetings in Accra; overthrown 1966; died 1972.
4) PAN-AFRICAN CONFERENCES (KEY ONES)

1st Pan-African Conference (1900)

  • Held in London (Westminster Town Hall), 23‚Äì25 July.
  • Aims: unite Africans, oppose colonization/exploitation, promote rights and economic interests.
  • Discussed: living conditions of blacks, human rights violations, racial discrimination.
  • Sent memo to the Queen demanding respect for black rights.

2nd Pan-African Conference (1919, Paris)

  • After World War I; linked to Paris Peace Conference.
  • Resolutions: protect Africans by international laws; land held in trust for Africans; stop exploitation; abolish slavery/capital punishment; right to education.

3rd Conference (1923)

  • Held in London and Lisbon; mainly diaspora; repeated earlier resolutions.

4th Conference (1927)

  • Held in New York; mainly African Americans.

5th Pan-African Conference (Manchester, 1945) ‚Äì VERY IMPORTANT

Why it was unique

  • Organized mainly by Africans from the continent (not just diaspora).
  • No white philanthropist sponsorship.
  • Trade unions from Africa participated.

Key leaders present

  • Du Bois (President), Nkrumah & Padmore (Secretaries), Kenyatta, Banda, etc.

Major resolutions

  • Win political power mainly through non-violent methods (strikes, boycotts).
  • African intellectuals must mobilize the masses.
  • Called strongly for African unity.

Significance

  • Turning point‚ÄîAfrican leaders returned home and led nationalist struggles (e.g., Nkrumah, Kenyatta, Banda).
5) ACHIEVEMENTS & WEAKNESSES OF PAN-AFRICANISM

Achievements

  • Created unity among Africans at home and in diaspora.
  • Restored dignity/confidence‚Äîencouraged pride in African identity.
  • Supported African nationalism and independence struggles.
  • Laid foundation for OAU (later AU).

Weaknesses/Challenges

  • Disagreements among leaders on strategy (e.g., Du Bois vs Washington).
  • Hostility from Europeans; arrests (e.g., Garvey).
  • Colonialism limited African participation (visas, repression).
  • Poverty, illiteracy and weak funding undermined projects.
  • Post-independence divisions (radical vs conservative; Francophone vs Anglophone).
6) FORMATION OF OAU (ORGANIZATION OF AFRICAN UNITY)

Background: Continental meetings after independence

  • Accra conferences (1958), Tunis (1960), Addis Ababa (1960).
  • Divisions arose over Congo crisis (Lumumba): radicals vs conservatives vs moderates.
  • Brazzaville (1960) ‚Äì conservative Francophone states.
  • Casablanca (1961) ‚Äì radicals.
  • Monrovia (1961) ‚Äì tried to unite groups.

Formation of OAU

  • Formed in May 1963 in Addis Ababa, under Emperor Haile Selassie I (first chairman).
  • Started with 32 states; grew to 54; admitted SADR (1984) leading to Morocco‚Äôs withdrawal.
  • Headquarters: Addis Ababa, Ethiopia.

Aims/Objectives of OAU

  1. Promote African unity
  2. Coordinate efforts to uplift Africans
  3. Eradicate colonialism
  4. Promote international co-operation
  5. Defend African sovereignty

Principles of OAU

  • Sovereign equality of states
  • Non-interference in internal affairs
  • Peaceful settlement of disputes
  • Respect borders/territorial integrity
  • Non-alignment
  • Condemn political assassination/subversion
  • Support decolonization

Organs/Structure of OAU

  1. Assembly of Heads of State & Government (supreme; yearly)
  2. Council of Ministers (foreign affairs ministers)
  3. Secretariat (Secretary-General; administrative work)
  4. Commission for Mediation, Conciliation and Arbitration

Some specialized committees/agencies

  • Liberation Committee (Dar es Salaam)
  • Economic & Social Commission
  • Defence Commission
  • Trade ministers conference
  • URTNA
  • Sports council, etc.

Achievements of OAU

  • Helped end colonial/minority rule (supported liberation movements).
  • Gave Africa one voice internationally (e.g., UN).
  • Mediated disputes (e.g., Kenya‚ÄìSomalia, Ethiopia‚ÄìSomalia).
  • Encouraged economic/technical cooperation; supported cultural ties (sports, media).

Weaknesses of OAU

  • Political instability/coups in member states.
  • Boundary disputes and national self-interest.
  • Foreign interference (Cold War politics).
  • No strong enforcement mechanism; no standing army.
  • Financial shortages.
  • Human rights abuses by leaders often ignored.
7) AFRICAN UNION (AU)

Origin/Formation

  • Sirte Declaration (Libya), 9 Sept 1999 called for African Union.
  • Constitutive Act adopted at Lom√© (2000); Lusaka (2001) paved the way.
  • AU launched in Durban, South Africa (2002).

Why OAU changed to AU

  • To speed up African integration and solve Africa‚Äôs social, economic and political problems, and strengthen Africa‚Äôs position in global economy.

How AU differs from OAU

  • AU allows intervention in member states during conflict or mass human rights violations (OAU emphasized non-interference).
  • AU involves African peoples more (Pan-African Parliament, advisory councils).
  • AU has stronger development agenda via NEPAD.
  • AU has accountability mechanism e.g., APRM (peer review).
  • AU proposes peace/security structures (Peace & Security Council).

Objectives of AU (short list)

  • Unity and solidarity
  • Defend sovereignty and territorial integrity
  • Promote peace, security and stability
  • Accelerate political and economic integration
  • Promote democracy, good governance and human rights
  • Promote Africa‚Äôs common positions internationally
  • Support development, science and technology
  • Improve health and fight preventable diseases
  • Coordinate policies of regional economic communities

Main organs of AU

  • Assembly
  • Executive Council
  • Commission
  • Permanent Representatives Committee
  • Court of Justice
  • Peace and Security Council
  • Pan-African Parliament
  • Economic, Social & Cultural Council
  • Financial institutions (African Central Bank, African Monetary Fund, African Investment Bank)
8) REGIONAL CO-OPERATION BODIES

A) EAST AFRICAN COMMUNITY (EAC)

(i) Early cooperation background

  • Court of Appeal (1902)
  • Postal Union (1911)
  • Customs Union (1917)
  • Currency Board (1920)
  • East Africa High Commission (EAHC) formed 1948 for common services.
  • Replaced by EACSO in 1961 (HQ Nairobi).

(ii) EAC (1967‚Äì1977)

  • Treaty signed 6 June 1967, effective 1 Dec 1967.
  • Members: Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania.

Aims

  • Promote trade and common market
  • Provide common services (railways, airways, posts, telecom)
  • Allow freer movement of people
  • Provide forum for economic/political discussion

Organs

  • East African Authority (Heads of State) ‚Äì supreme
  • East African Legislative Assembly
  • Secretariat (Arusha)
  • Common Market Council & Tribunal

Why it collapsed (1977)

  • Suspicion Kenya dominated (stronger economy)
  • Personality clashes (e.g., Nyerere vs Idi Amin)
  • Ideological differences (Tanzania socialism vs Kenya/Uganda capitalism)
  • Uganda instability and failure to pay obligations
  • Different development priorities (rail vs roads)
  • Inadequate funds (EADB weakness)
  • Border closures + Uganda‚ÄìTanzania war (1978‚Äì79) was final blow

(iii) Rebirth of EAC (1999/2000/2001)

  • Treaty signed 30 Nov 1999 (Arusha); in force 7 July 2000; launched 15 Jan 2001.

Organs (new EAC)

  • Summit
  • Council of Ministers
  • Coordinating Committee
  • Sectoral Committees
  • Court of Justice
  • Legislative Assembly
  • Secretariat

Challenges (new EAC)

  • Suspicion of Kenya‚Äôs dominance
  • Smuggling
  • Harassment disputes (e.g., fishermen on Lake Victoria)
  • Cattle raids (Kenya‚ÄìUganda border)
  • Overlapping membership in other bodies (COMESA, SADC)

Achievements

  • East African passport boosts movement
  • Leaders‚Äô forum for negotiation
  • Improved transport links
  • Increased interaction among professionals/civil society
  • Reduced tariffs on industrial goods; promotes trade

B) ECOWAS

Meaning/Formation

  • West African regional group; treaty signed Lagos, 28 May 1975.
  • Members include: Nigeria, Ghana, Senegal, Ivory Coast, Sierra Leone, Liberia, etc.

Objectives

  • Liberalize trade and improve relations
  • Promote industrial development
  • Cooperate in transport, agriculture, communications, social and cultural affairs
  • Create a customs union
  • Improve living standards
  • Encourage cultural interaction

Organs

  • Authority of Heads of State (highest)
  • Council of Ministers
  • Executive Secretariat (Lagos)
  • Tribunal
  • Specialized commissions

Challenges

  • Large area‚Äîhard to coordinate
  • Francophone vs Anglophone differences
  • Suspicion of powerful states (Nigeria)
  • Border closures/violations of treaty
  • Foreign interference
  • Poor transport/communication
  • Political instability and conflicts

Achievements

  • Wider market; reduced tariffs
  • Encouraged infrastructure development
  • Free movement (visa waivers)
  • ECOMOG helped stop civil wars
  • Education progress (joint syllabus/exams)

C) COMESA

Meaning/Formation

  • Common Market for Eastern & Southern Africa.
  • Formed 8 Dec 1994, replacing PTA (1981).

Why formed

  • Individual states‚Äô efforts after independence were weak; needed joint market and joint use of resources for growth.

Objectives (short)

  • Promote regional growth & development
  • Peace, security and stability
  • Balanced and harmonious development of production/marketing
  • Strengthen relations with rest of world; common positions internationally
  • Create enabling environment for investment

Principles (selected)

  • Equality and interdependence
  • Solidarity and self-reliance
  • Non-aggression
  • Cooperation and policy harmonization
  • Democracy, rule of law, accountability
  • Peaceful dispute settlement
  • Human rights protection

Structure

  • Authority (Heads of State)
  • Council of Ministers
  • Court of Justice
  • Committee of Central Bank Governors
  • Intergovernmental Committee
  • Technical Committees
  • Secretariat (Lusaka, Zambia)
  • Consultative Committee (business/stakeholders)

Achievements

  • Trade liberalization in region
  • Computerized customs systems being introduced
  • Improved transport/communication projects
  • Legal framework encouraging private sector investment
  • Harmonization of macro-economic/monetary policies
  • Large market (about 385 million people) boosts industry and employment

Challenges

  • Divided loyalty (members also in EAC/SADC)
  • Personality differences among leaders
  • Trade disputes (e.g., duty-free products conflicts)
  • Poor transport networks
  • Unfair competition from subsidized Western farm products
  • Civil wars and boundary conflicts
